<work>

Newsroom & team tools: I've been building a lot of internal tools lately, for both news app team use and for newsroom reporter & editor use. I've started putting together an AJC digital design guide for the news app team to implement consistent styling that matches our print styles. I've taken a front-end project template built by a coworker that utilized grunt as a task manager and bower as a dependency manager, enhanced the template, and built a grunt-init template generator around it to scaffold out a front-end codebase everyone can use. I've also built two grunt plugins to pull data from external sources and convert to JSON to work with in the project template – grunt-google-archieml parses Google Docs using ArchieML and grunt-sql-bakery connects to sql databases and pulls down json from any tables or views you configure to your grunt project. For newsroom use, I built a tool called QuoteIt, largely inspired by NPR Visuals' Quotable tool (and inspired by their open sourced code), which allows for anyone in the newsroom to create shareable quote images to go with their stories on social media platforms without having to install Photoshop or the AJC brand fonts on their computers.

news applications: The AJC's home for Atlanta crime data, Crime in ATL, was designed, developed, researched and is maintained by me. I built the Ruby on Rails app that scrapes the Atlanta Police Department website nightly, stores the data in a MySQL database and renders the data in a REST API. The API is then interpreted by a separate single-page javascript application I built using Backbone & Marionette to power the front-end of the site. The Georgia Legislative Navigator is built in a similar fashion – Ruby on Rails API to emit the data, Backbone/Marionette application powering the front-end – but I only contributed to the front-end design and some of the development for the legislative navigator over the last two legislative sessions.

longform story packages: My first longform interactive project, War in our backyards, was also one of my most successful. I researched the historical data (with some assistance), and designed and developed the entire site (with the exception of most of the map page, which was built by another developer). War in our backyards won an SND Award of Excellence, won "Best Multi-platform Coverage of a Single Story" and was chosen as "Story of the Year" at the 2015 Georgia APME Awards, and was a finalist at the 2015 Atlanta Press Club awards. Another project I designed and developed alongside another AJC news apps team member, Forgotten Memories, also racked up a few awards this year; the project received an SND award of excellence, was a finalist at the Atlanta Press Club awards, and was awarded a first place prize for integrated storytelling by the Society of Features Journalism. More recently, I've been designing and developing smaller story packages, like this investigation surrounding a Georgia prison doctor.

graphics + data viz: Data visualization is a large part of my job, but often results in not-so-exciting simple charts, graphs and maps. Here's a few examples of the more interesting graphics and visualizations I've built. Following the Charleston shooting in June 2015, I worked with our team's summer intern to design and develop this mass shootings package of visualizations exploring a half century of shootings, using data collected by the Stanford's Geospatial Center. This Atlanta Forward package compares Atlanta "by the numbers" with Charlotte and Dallas, and was published at the start of a year-long initiative looking at the current state of Atlanta's growth and development in comparison to the rest of the nation. The first project I published at the AJC as a news apps developer was an Exploring Obamacare "choose your own adventure" tool to let readers explore how the Obamacare subsides and the healthcare marketplace would effect them.

You can see more of the projects I've been committing to on github.
